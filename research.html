Rising markups in the banking industry – with Cristián Sánchez and Carlos Burga

The Industrial Organization literature has traditionally examined market power in specific contexts and timeframes, with limited focus on its evolution over time. While rising markups have been documented in industries such as cereals, cement, and airlines, empirical research on credit markets remains scarce. This study addresses this gap by estimating markups in the Chilean credit market using confidential loan-level data from 2013 to 2019. We develop a structural model that incorporates borrowers' bank choice, loan size, and repayment probability to estimate demand elasticities, marginal costs, and markups. Our findings reveal an 8.7% increase in market power over the sample period, with larger firms facing markups approximately 17% higher than smaller firms. Additionally, marginal costs decreased by 11%, while firms' price sensitivity declined by 13%. These results highlight the interplay between market concentration, price elasticity, and financial stability, offering valuable insights for policymakers and stakeholders in the banking industry.

The effect of female role models on STEM major choice

This research analyzes how female role models (professors, teaching assistants and classmates), can affect major choice among female Civil Engineering students at the University of Chile. Using a conditional logit model, I estimate how exposure to female role models in the years prior to the decision influences the probability of choosing each academic major. Results show that female students attending mathematics or computer science courses taught by female professors are more likely to choose the mathematics and computer science majors, respectively. This effect is only observed for professors and not teaching assistants or classmates. Counterfactual analysis shows that increasing the number of female professors within majors where women are underrepresented, may encourage them to join these majors. The results also suggest that strengthening women's mathematics preparation may have comparable effects.
